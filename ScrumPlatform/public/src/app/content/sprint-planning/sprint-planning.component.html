Sprint Planning

What is Sprint Planning

The Scrum Team gathers to discuss the work to be accomplished in the upcoming Sprint in the form of a Sprint Goal. Then, the development Team will determine how the work will be accomplished by creating a sufficiently detailed plan called the Sprint Backlog.

The Sprint Planning meeting is broken down into 2 Parts:

  • The "What" - Set of PBI selected by the Development Team, accompanied by a short statement providing an objective for the Sprint - The Sprint Goal.

  • The "How" -PBI selected for this Sprint in additiona to the short range, detailed plan to deliver them - The Sprint Backlog

Goal:

  • Development Team discusses suggested PBI with Product Owner

  • Development Team selects PBI to accomplish in the upcoming Sprint

  • Development Team creates their plan, called the Sprint Backlog

Roster:

  • Development Team

  • Product Owner

  • Scrum Master

  • SMEs

Time-Box:

The length of the sprint will dictate how long a sprint planning meeting should take and it is the Scrum Master’s responsibility to keep the meeting on track and within the time limit. Normally 2 to 4 hours; maximum of 8 hours for a 4-week Sprint

Frequency:

At the start of each Sprint the Product Owner provides product backlog at the beginning of the meeting and gives an explanation of the objectives and items in the backlog. The dev team will go up and move each item into the sprint from the product backlog. Items moved over should be completed during the sprint.The dev team decides which items can be reasonably completed during the sprint.Each item moved into the sprint column should then be broken down into specific tasks and placed in the Sprint BacklogRefined product backlog items are important for effective sprints. Any member of the dev team should be able to pick up a task and know exactly what is needed to complete the task and what constitutes “done” for the task.During the breakdown of each sprint item into sprint backlog tasks the team may call in the product owner or scrum master to get clarification on expectations or further explanation of what an item means.Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Dev Team need to agree on an overarching goal that will help maintain a cohesiveness.

Crafting the Sprint Goal

When crafting a Sprint Goal, the Scrum Team comes together for a few distinct conversations:

First, the Product Owner discusses the objective for the next Sprint should achieve and suggests a set of Product Backlog Items that would achieve it.

The Development Team then selects a number of these suggested items to accomplish in the upcoming Sprint. This selection is solely up to the members of the Development Team.

The Scrum Team as a whole then crafts a Sprint Goal, which is an objective (often one to two sentences) that will be met within the Sprint along with the set of selected Backlog Items.

Example:

The PO can suggest PBIs but the development team does not have to accept all the PBIs and can choose which one they will complete with the given timeframe and difficulty.

Responsiblities:

  • Product Owner- Suggest a set of PBI for upcoming Sprint and discussess what the Sprint would achieve. Collaborates with the Development Team on the crafting the Sprint Goal Answers questions on selected PBI and makes trade-offs when needed *Can only remove selected PBI*

  • Development Team- Collaborates with Product Owner on crafting the Sprint Goal Selects PBI from suggested items to complete in the upcoming Sprint Creates a sufficiently detailed plan to accomplish the selected PBI called the Sprint Backlog *Can add, remove, and modify items (PBI / tasks) at any time*

  • Scrum Master- Creates an environment centered on collaboration and open communication Ensures that the Sprint Goal is understood by teh Product Owner and Development Team Facilitates the discussions that allow the Development Team create the Sprint Backlog as a group *Can suggest missing items to fulfill the Definition of Done*

Now for a quick Exercise

The group will evaluate the tasks and determine whether any of the tasks are too broad and should be broken down into more specific tasks. For example, if the product backlog list contains the items below, then the group may decide that “Prep the room for painting” should be broken down further. The task could be broken into “Move the furniture”, “Lay down tarps”, and “Tape off painting area”.

The product backlog would then look like:

  • Measure the room

  • Decide on a color

  • Get supplies

  • Move the furniture

  • Lay down tarps

  • Tape off painting area

  • Paint the room

  • Clean up
